Local Food

1. Cawaki

Cawaki is the local name for sea urchin, the only edible species urchin in Fiji. The sea urchins in Yasawa are harvested from the crystal-clear waters surrounding the islands. Here, they are prepared in a variety of ways to highlight their unique taste. Whether enjoyed fresh, straight from the sea, or as part of a gourmet dish, sea urchin adds a touch of luxury and local authenticity to Yasawan dining. The sea urchins are often served raw to preserve their delicate flavor. They might be featured in traditional dishes, on sushi, or as an elegant appetizer. Local chefs take pride in presenting sea urchin in ways that showcase its rich, briny sweetness, often paired with complementary ingredients like fresh herbs, citrus, or local seaweed.

2. Kokoda

Kokoda is the local name for Fijian ceviche. Kokoda is a refreshing and flavorful dish made from raw fish that is marinated in citrus juices, usually lime or lemon, and then mixed with a variety of fresh ingredients. The dish is popular in Fiji and other Pacific Island nations, where it’s a staple in local cuisine. Kokoda is a traditional Fijian dish made with fresh raw fish, typically snapper or tuna, which is diced and marinated in tangy lime or lemon juice to “cook” it through. After several hours of marination, the fish is mixed with chopped vegetables such as tomatoes, onions, and bell peppers, and sometimes enhanced with creamy coconut milk for added richness. The mixture is seasoned with fresh herbs like cilantro, salt, and pepper to taste. The result is a vibrant and refreshing dish, often served chilled as a light appetizer or main course that highlights the fresh, bright flavors of the Pacific Islands.

3. Nama

Nama is also known as sea grapes or seaweed. The Nama is commonly served in ways that highlight its fresh, crunchy texture and briny flavor. It is often featured in salads, where the sea grapes are cleaned and mixed with diced tomatoes, onions, and cucumbers, dressed with a light vinaigrette or tangy citrus dressing that complements its natural taste. In some variations, nama is combined with coconut milk, creating a creamy dish that may include chopped herbs, lime juice, and seasonings, enhancing its richness and flavor. Nama can also be served as a side dish alongside main courses, paired with local seafood to provide a refreshing contrast. Additionally, it may be used as a garnish or accompaniment, adding color and a burst of flavor to various dishes. In Yasawa, nama is cherished for its fresh taste and versatility in simple, vibrant local cuisine.

4. Kaikoso

Kaikoso is also referred to as a shellfich delight. Kaikoso is a traditional Fijian delicacy consisting of small, edible shellfish found in the local waters. Often served in its shell, kaikoso is cherished for its tender, slightly sweet meat. This shellfish is typically steamed, grilled, or cooked in a flavorful broth. In Fijian cuisine, kaikoso may be enjoyed in a variety of dishes, sometimes combined with local ingredients such as coconut milk, herbs, and spices to enhance its delicate, briny flavor. Whether featured in a seafood platter or a savory stew, kaikoso offers a taste of the rich marine bounty of Fiji, highlighting the region’s culinary traditions and fresh, oceanic flavors.

Tavioka

Tavioka is also referred to as Cassava. Cassava is a starchy root vegetable native to South America, but it's widely cultivated in tropical regions around the world. Its scientific name is Manihot esculenta. Cassava is a major source of carbohydrates and is often used to make tapioca, flour, and various dishes. It's known for its high calorie content and versatility in cooking. However, raw cassava contains cyanogenic compounds and must be properly processed before consumption to remove these toxins. Cassava is famous in the Yasawa Islands and all parts of Fiji because it’s a staple food that plays a crucial role in the local diet and culture. The islanders cultivate cassava for its versatility and nutritional value, using it in various traditional dishes. It’s also easy to grow in the tropical climate, making it an important crop for food security. Additionally, cassava holds cultural significance, often being featured in celebrations and community gatherings.

Uvi

Uvi is also referred to as yams. Yams have a rough, bark-like skin, and they are typically much larger than sweet potatoes. They are starchy and used in a variety of traditional dishes across Africa, the Caribbean, and Asia. They can be boiled, roasted, or fried and are often served with savory meals. These starchy tubers, with their rough brown skin and rich earthy flavor, are a staple food that has been enjoyed for generations. Uvi are versatile and nutritious root vegetables that are larger and starchier than sweet potatoes. They are traditionally grown across the Yasawas, often featured in local Fijian dishes. Uvi can be boiled, roasted, or mashed, and are perfect for hearty meals. Whether you're a local or visiting the Yasawa Islands, trying dishes made with Uvi is a must! These yams are typically served with seafood, coconut milk, or even grilled meats, offering a delicious taste of Fijian tradition.

Kumala

Kumala is also referred to as sweet potato. Kumala are delicious, nutrient-packed tubers that come in a variety of colors, from orange to purple. They are sweeter and softer than yams (uvi), making them a perfect choice for both savory and sweet dishes. In the Yasawas, Kumala is grown locally and enjoyed in many forms. Sweet potatoes are not only tasty but also highly nutritious. They are rich in vitamins A and C, making them a great choice for a balanced, healthy diet. Kumala has been a key part of the Yasawan diet for generations, providing energy and sustenance for daily life. In the Yasawa Islands, Kumala is often boiled, roasted, or mashed and served alongside fish, meats, or coconut-based dishes. Whether you're enjoying a traditional Fijian feast or looking for a simple snack, Kumala brings a naturally sweet and satisfying flavor to the table.

Bele

Bele is also known as bele spinach. Bele-spinach is one of the most popular leafy green vegetables worldwide, valued for its mild flavor and impressive nutritional profile. Known for its tender, vibrant green leaves, spinach is a versatile ingredient used in everything from salads to smoothies, soups, and stir-fries. It is a cool-weather leafy green with smooth or slightly crinkled leaves. Its delicate, mild taste makes it easy to incorporate into a wide range of dishes. Whether eaten raw or cooked, spinach is an excellent source of vitamins and minerals, making it a go-to choice for health-conscious eaters.

A Cultural Perspective

Traditional clothes are outfits that belong to a specific culture or community. They often reflect the history and customs of that group. Made from local materials, these clothes are usually worn during special events or ceremonies.Here are some chlothes that often worn in the Yasawa's

Sulu Chamba

Since Fiji is mostly Christian, most people dress conservatively. For women, that means wearing what they call a "sulu chamba". A sulu chamba is basically a dress with a skirt that goes down to your feet and is normally brightly colored with an islandish design.

Sulu vakataga/Bula shirt

The Sulu Vakataga is an iconic piece of clothing worn by Fijian men. It is a type of wrap-around skirt that is both practical and symbolic, representing Fijian heritage and culture. Worn with pride across the Yasawa Islands and all over Fiji, the Sulu Vakataga is perfect for both casual and formal occasions. The Sulu Vakataga is a tailored wrap-around garment typically knee-length, secured with buttons or a tie at the waist. It comes in a variety of colors and patterns, from plain fabrics to intricate designs representing Fijian culture. Whether for traditional ceremonies, work, or leisure, the Sulu Vakataga is a versatile and respected part of Fijian fashion. Fijian men wear the Sulu Vakataga in many settings—government offices, schools, churches, or village gatherings. It's especially common during cultural ceremonies, where it pairs with a formal shirt. While modern in appearance, the Sulu Vakataga remains deeply tied to Fijian identity and history.

Sulu-i-ra

The Sarong, a lightweight, wrap-around cloth, is a timeless piece of clothing worn across the tropical regions of the world, including Fiji. Known for its vibrant patterns and versatility, the sarong is a favorite among both men and women in the Yasawa Islands. It can be worn as a skirt, dress, or even as a beach cover-up, offering both style and comfort. A sarong is a large, rectangular piece of fabric, often made of cotton or silk, and adorned with colorful designs such as flowers, island motifs, or geometric patterns. In Fiji, the sarong is commonly worn by both locals and tourists alike, thanks to its breathable fabric and adaptable style, perfect for the warm island climate. Sarongs are incredibly versatile. They can be tied around the waist to be worn as a skirt (similar to the Sulu Vakataga), draped over the shoulders as a dress, or used as a cover-up at the beach. Its ease of use and light, flowing fabric make the sarong ideal for both casual island outings and relaxed beach days. For men, it’s often worn in a simpler wrap style around the waist, while women might fashion it into a halter dress or a chic wraparound skirt. The sarong is a must-have for anyone in Fiji, especially when visiting the pristine beaches of the Yasawa Islands. Its versatility allows it to be used in various settings—from lounging by the sea to attending a casual evening gathering. Whether it’s bright florals or understated island tones, the sarong’s designs reflect the beauty and spirit of the islands.
